The Core Idea

The original Moneyball insight was simple: the market mispriced on-base percentage because scouts didn't value it. In 2026, raw WAR is widely tracked. But WAR stability is not. A player who produces 5 WAR every single year is far more valuable to a team than one who alternates between 0 and 10, yet the market often pays them the same.

Minimum WAR across all seasons. Measures the worst-case scenario. A player who never falls below 4 WAR is invaluable in roster construction.

Inverted log salary. Low-salary players with strong profiles score higher here. Captures the gap between production and how much the market has noticed.

Expected salary (Avg WAR × $8M) minus actual salary. Directly measures how underpaid a player is relative to their production history.

Linear slope of WAR over seasons. Rewards improving players. A young player trending up is worth more than an older one trending down.

Ratio of qualified seasons to expected seasons. A player who is consistently on the field is more reliable than one with repeated injury absences.

Their rank reflects genuine WAR stability, but the salary discount is structural, not a true market inefficiency. Focus on their consistency score and WAR floor rather than the overall composite.

Model Methodology How the Hidden Value Score is calculated ▼

The Hidden Value Score is a composite metric (0 to 1) that identifies batters who are both undervalued by the market and reliable producers. It combines six components, each normalized to a 0–1 range across all players in the dataset.

Formula

Score = (Consistency × 0.30) + (WAR Floor × 0.20) + (Market Recognition × 0.20) + (Salary Gap × 0.15) + (WAR Trend × 0.10) + (Availability × 0.05)

Components

30% Consistency

Avg WAR / (1 + Std Dev WAR). Rewards stable year-over-year production. A player averaging 5 WAR every year scores higher than one alternating between 2 and 8.

20% WAR Floor

Minimum single-season WAR across all analyzed seasons. Measures downside risk. A player who never had a bad season ranks higher than one with a volatile floor.

20% Market Recognition

Inverted log-salary relative to the dataset maximum. Low salary = high recognition score. Captures how much of a player’s true value the market has yet to price in.

15% Salary Gap

(Avg WAR × $8M) − Actual Salary, in $M. Directly measures the dollar gap between what a player is worth at market rate and what they are actually paid.

10% WAR Trend

Linear slope of WAR over time (least-squares). A positive slope means the player is improving season over season. Negative = declining trajectory.

5% Availability

Qualified seasons / expected seasons in the window. A player active for 4 out of 4 seasons scores 1.0. Injuries that caused missed seasons reduce this component.

Key Findings

Four types of hidden value emerge from the data.

The Most Consistent Hitters in Baseball

Yainer Diaz (HOU) leads the full dataset with a WAR standard deviation of just 0.22 across 3 seasons. Among players with 5+ avg WAR, Kyle Tucker (CHC) stands out at 0.48 std dev over 4 seasons: 5.5, 5.5, 4.6, 4.5. You know exactly what you're getting. Will Smith (LAD, 0.43 std) is even more consistent but at a lower output level.

Biggest Salary Gap: Jarren Duran

Jarren Duran (BOS) is paid $3.75M and averages 5.37 WAR over 3 seasons. His expected salary at market rate is $42.96M, a gap of $39.21M. His 2024 season (9.0 WAR) inflates the variance, but even his floor (2.2 WAR) is valuable at that price.

Under the Radar: Ernie Clement

Ernie Clement (TOR, $1.97M) is the clearest market inefficiency. Two qualified seasons, 3.3 and 4.3 WAR, with virtually no variance. At $1.97M for that level of production, he represents exactly the kind of player Moneyball was built to find.

Most Overpaid by This Model

Nick Castellanos (PHI, $20M) scores last: 0.30 avg WAR with a negative floor of −0.8 WAR in 2025. Carlos Correa ($36M) and Starling Marte ($19.5M) also land at the bottom, combining declining production with high variance and large salaries.

Consistency vs. Salary

The sweet spot is the bottom-right: high consistency score (reliable production) combined with low salary. These are the players the market has not yet priced in. Hover for details.

So What?
The Model Identified Real Value Before the Market Did

The strongest validation of this model is contractual, not statistical. Gunnar Henderson, identified as the top hidden value player at $782K, signed a new deal worth $8.5M in 2026. Cal Raleigh went from $1M to $11M. Zach Neto and Brice Turang each received their first significant arbitration raises. In each case, the model flagged the undervaluation before the market corrected it.

Beyond the pre-arb cases, the treatment of Aaron Judge at rank 3 (score 0.713) is the most analytically interesting result. At $40M per year, Judge is the highest-paid player in the dataset, yet his 9.0 avg WAR over 4 seasons with a floor of 7.5 WAR makes him genuinely undervalued even at that salary. The model correctly identifies that consistency at elite production levels is worth more than the market typically prices in on long-term deals. This is a real signal, not a pre-arb artifact.

The honest caveat remains: the salary gap component inflates scores for pre-arb players in ways that reflect MLB labor rules (service time, pre-arbitration salary caps) rather than true market inefficiency. The most operationally useful output is the Consistency Score applied to players in their first two arbitration years, where the gap between market salary and true value is largest and most actionable for a front office building competitively on a budget.

Glossary

Moneyball 2025
WAR
Wins Above Replacement. Estimates how many wins a player adds over a replacement-level player. 2 = average, 5+ = All-Star, 8+ = MVP-caliber.
Hidden Value Score
Composite score (0 to 1) combining consistency, WAR floor, market recognition, salary gap, WAR trend, and availability. Higher = better hidden value.
Consistency Score
Core metric of this model: Avg WAR / (1 + Std Dev). Rewards reliable year-over-year production. Higher = more stable output.
Std Dev (WAR)
Standard deviation of a player's WAR across seasons. Low std dev = consistent output; high std dev = volatile, boom-or-bust performance.
WAR Floor
The lowest single-season WAR a player posted across the analyzed window. Reflects downside risk: how bad was their worst year?
WAR Trend
Linear slope of WAR over time (positive = improving, negative = declining). Computed via least-squares regression on season-by-season WAR values.
Salary Gap
Expected salary (Avg WAR x $8M) minus actual salary in $M. A larger gap means the player is more underpaid relative to their production.
Market Recognition
How much the market has already priced in a player's talent, derived from their salary level. Low recognition = the market has not caught up yet.
CV (Coefficient of Variation)
Standard deviation divided by the mean. Measures relative variability across different production levels. Used internally to normalize consistency.
Pre-Arb
Pre-Arbitration. Players in their first ~3 years of MLB service, earning near the league minimum (~$760K-$800K in 2025).
