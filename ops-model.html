How It Works

The model learns from 4 seasons of MLB batting data (2022–2025). For each player, it looks at recent performance trends, age trajectory, and playing time to predict next-season OPS. Only players with 300+ plate appearances are included. Below that threshold, stats become too noisy to be reliable.

What We're Predicting

Next-season OPS (On-base Plus Slugging): combines how often a player gets on base with their power output. A score above .800 is excellent; below .700 is below average.

What the Model Uses

Current OPS, 2-season rolling average OPS_avg2 (to smooth outlier seasons), next-season age, WAR (overall value), and plate appearances PA.

The Algorithm

Linear regression: the simplest predictive model. Trained on 80% of the data, tested on the remaining 20% it has never seen. Measured by RMSE (average error) and R² (explained variance).

Data Source

Baseball Reference standard batting stats, seasons 2022–2025. Minimum 300 plate appearances per season to ensure sample size reliability.

Model Methodology Variables, coefficients, and technical limitations ▼

The model is a multivariate linear regression trained on 4 seasons of MLB batting data (2022–2025). It predicts next-season OPS from 5 input features derived from a player’s recent history.

Input Features

X1OPS (previous season)

Raw OPS from the season immediately before the prediction year. Strongest single predictor due to year-to-year autocorrelation (~0.60).

X22-season rolling avg OPS

Average OPS over the two most recent seasons. Smooths outlier years and captures underlying true-talent level better than a single season.

X3Plate Appearances

Total PA in the previous season. Controls for playing time: a 650-PA season carries more signal than a 300-PA season. Also proxies for injury history.

X4Age

Player age entering the prediction season. Captures aging curves: production typically peaks at 27–29 and declines thereafter.

X5WAR (previous season)

Baseball Reference WAR. Adds defensive and baserunning context that OPS alone misses, distinguishing all-around contributors from pure hitters.

Known Limitations

!No Statcast inputs

Exit velocity, launch angle, and xwOBA are not included. These are stronger predictors but reduce sample size significantly.

!Injury blind

The model cannot predict injuries. PA from the prior year is a weak proxy for health.

!Small training set

134 training observations is modest. R² of 0.21 means 79% of variance is unexplained, which is honest but limiting.

!Mean regression bias

Like all regression models, it systematically underestimates elite performers and overestimates below-average ones.

The model is intentionally simple and transparent. It is designed to demonstrate an analytical approach, not to compete with proprietary systems used by MLB front offices.

Under the Hood: How a Prediction Is Made

Let's walk through exactly how the model predicted Juan Soto's 2025 OPS, step by step. No black box.

Step 1. Gather Inputs

Juan Soto's 2024 season: OPS = 0.921, PA = 715, WAR = 6.1, age in 2025 = 26. Rolling 2-season average: OPS_avg2 = 0.884 (avg. of 2023 and 2024).

Step 2. Apply the Formula

The model learned these weights from the data:
OPS × 0.39 + OPS_avg2 × 0.24 + Age × −0.003 + WAR × 0.006 + PA × 0.0001 + 0.31

Step 3. Compute

(0.921 × 0.39) + (0.884 × 0.24) + (26 × −0.003) + (6.1 × 0.006) + (715 × 0.0001) + 0.31
= 0.925

Step 4. Compare to Reality

Soto's actual 2025 OPS: 0.921. The model predicted 0.925, an error of just +0.004. One of the model's most accurate predictions.

Note: current OPS is the strongest predictor (coefficient 0.39), followed by the 2-season average (0.24). Age has a small negative effect (−0.003 per year), consistent with the well-documented aging curve in baseball.

What This Model Can't Do (Yet)

A model is only as honest as its limitations. Here's what's missing, and what comes next.

R² of 0.209

The model explains ~21% of the variance in next-season OPS. That sounds low, and it is. But baseball performance is genuinely hard to predict. Professional models (ZiPS, Steamer) reach ~40–50% with far more data.

Small Training Set

Only 134 training samples. Players need 3+ qualifying seasons to be included. More historical data (pre-2022) would significantly improve the model's stability.

No Statcast Data

Exit velocity, launch angle, and barrel rate are among the strongest predictors of future performance. They capture "luck-independent" skill. Not yet included here.

No Park Adjustments

A player at Coors Field (hitter-friendly) inflates their OPS significantly vs. a pitcher's park. Without park factors, some projections will be systematically off.

What's Next

Add Statcast features, park factors, platoon splits, and test Ridge/Lasso regularization. Ultimately, build a full projection system comparable to ZiPS or Steamer.

Glossary

OPS Prediction Model
OPS
On-base Plus Slugging. Combines On-Base Percentage (OBP) and Slugging Percentage (SLG). Range: ~0.600 (poor) to 1.000+ (elite). League average ~.720.
WAR
Wins Above Replacement. Estimates how many wins a player adds over a replacement-level player. 2 = average, 5+ = All-Star, 8+ = MVP-caliber.
PA
Plate Appearances. Total times a batter comes to the plate, including walks and hit-by-pitches. More complete than at-bats (AB).
OBP
On-Base Percentage. Fraction of PAs in which the batter reaches base (hits + walks + HBP). League average ~.320.
SLG
Slugging Percentage. Total bases divided by at-bats. Measures raw power output. League average ~.400.
RMSE
Root Mean Square Error. Average prediction error in the same unit as the target (here, OPS). Lower is better. RMSE of 0.073 means predictions are off by ~73 OPS points on average.
R²
R-squared. Proportion of variance in the outcome explained by the model. Ranges 0 to 1. R² of 0.21 means the model explains 21% of the variation in next-season OPS.
Linear Regression
A statistical model that finds the best-fit relationship between input variables and an outcome. Used here to predict next-season OPS from recent hitting stats.
Residual
The difference between a predicted value and the actual value. Positive residual = model underestimated; negative = overestimated.
