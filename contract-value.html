How It Works

The core idea: every WAR has a dollar value on the open market. If a player produces more value than they cost, they're a bargain. If they produce less, they're overpaid. This analysis pins market rate at $8M per WAR — the approximate 2025 free agent rate — and measures each player's surplus.

Step 1 — Collect Data

Batting stats (WAR, OPS, PA) from Baseball Reference. Salaries from Spotrac. Merged on player name. Only batters with 300+ PA included.

Step 2 — Market Rate

Set $/WAR = $8M based on 2025 free agent contracts. This is what teams pay per win on the open market — the benchmark for fair value.

Step 3 — Surplus Value

Surplus = (WAR × $8M) − Salary. Positive = team gets a bargain. Negative = player is overpaid relative to production.

Step 4 — Interpret

Young players on pre-arb contracts dominate the "best value" list. Veterans on long-term deals often show negative surplus — the cost of aging curves.

Key Findings

Three patterns stand out immediately from the data.

Best Value in Baseball

Geraldo Perdomo (ARI) earns $2.55M while producing 7.1 WAR — a surplus of $54.3M. At $0.36M/WAR, he's the most underpaid everyday player in MLB. Bobby Witt Jr. follows at $49.8M surplus before his contract extension kicks in.

Worst Contractual Decisions

Carlos Correa (MIN) produced just 0.2 WAR on a $36M salary — $34.4M in wasted money. Jose Altuve ($30M, 0.6 WAR) and Mike Trout ($35.4M, 1.7 WAR) also represent massive negative surplus driven by injuries and age.

The Ohtani Exception

Shohei Ohtani appears overpaid at −$17.2M surplus — but only because this analysis covers his batting only. His pitching adds substantial WAR not captured here. His true contract value is likely strongly positive when both roles are combined.
