How It Works

Statcast tracks every batted ball: its exit velocity, launch angle, and direction. From these physical measurements, MLB calculates expected stats (xBA, xSLG, xwOBA) that represent what a player should have produced based on contact quality alone, independent of luck.

Exit Velocity

Speed of the ball off the bat in mph. Higher exit velocity = harder contact = higher probability of hits and extra bases. League average is ~88 mph; elite is 92+ mph.

Launch Angle

Vertical angle of the ball leaving the bat. The sweet spot is 10°–30° (line drives + fly balls). Too low = grounders. Too high = pop-ups. Barrel zone: 26–30° + 98+ mph.

Expected Stats (xwOBA)

Weighted on-base average calculated from contact quality. Compared to actual wOBA, the gap reveals luck: wOBA − xwOBA < 0 = unlucky. > 0 = lucky (regression risk).

Barrel Rate

% of batted balls that are "barreled" (ideal exit velocity and launch angle combined). A barrel produces a .500+ BA and 1.500+ SLG. Elite: 12%+. League avg: ~7%.

Model Methodology Luck gap calculation, regression thresholds, and 2025→2026 validation ▼

This analysis quantifies how much luck affected each player’s 2025 results, then tests whether those luck signals predicted real 2026 performance. The core premise: actual wOBA minus expected xwOBA measures outcomes that contact quality alone cannot explain.

The Luck Gap Formula

Luck Gap = actual wOBA − xwOBA  (Statcast, min 100 PA)

A positive luck gap means a player outperformed their contact quality. A negative gap means they underperformed it. Both are expected to partially reverse.

Regression Thresholds

+Unlucky / Deserves Better

Luck gap < −0.015. Player significantly underperformed their xwOBA. Expect a positive correction as outcomes normalize.

−Lucky / Regression Risk

Luck gap > +0.015. Player significantly outperformed their xwOBA. Expect a pullback as BABIP and sequencing normalize.

~No Signal

Luck gap within ±0.015. Contact quality and results roughly aligned. No strong regression prediction.

Age Group Interpretation

≤25Young

Development noise is high. Luck gaps may reflect skill acquisition. Regression predictions are less reliable for this group.

26–30Prime

Most stable range. True talent is established and large gaps most strongly indicate impending regression.

31–35Veteran

Aging curves complicate interpretation. A negative luck gap may reflect genuine decline rather than bad luck.

36+Aging

Decline is the prior. Positive gaps are unlikely to sustain; negative gaps may not fully recover.

Statcast expected stats are computed by MLB using a Random Forest model. The luck gap layer adds a time-series dimension: do 2025 gaps predict 2026 movements? At 65%, they do, meaningfully above the 50% random baseline, but single-year predictions remain inherently noisy.

Key Findings

Three patterns stand out from the 2025 Statcast data. Read alongside age, the picture becomes much clearer. All predictions are validated against 2026 live results below.

Most Unlucky: Strong Rebound Expected

Juan Soto (26, Prime) hits 93.8 mph with 18.1% barrel rate (elite contact), yet his wOBA (.390) trails his xwOBA (.429) by 39 points. At 26 and in his physical peak, this gap is almost entirely luck. A significant bounce-back is near-certain.

Dansby Swanson (31, Veteran) and Corey Seager (31, Veteran) also show large negative gaps despite solid contact metrics. Unlucky in their prime, not declining.

Biggest Regression Risks: Age Matters

Miguel Andujar (30, Prime) posts a wOBA 56 points above his xwOBA with only 88 mph exit velocity and 4.7% barrel rate. Pure luck, and at 30 he has no youth excuse. Sharp decline incoming.

Jose Altuve (35, Veteran) overperforms by 31 points with below-average contact quality. At 35, that gap is unlikely to sustain. Aging curve combined with regression is a dangerous combination.

Age Changes Everything

An unlucky young player (≤25) is a buy-low gem. Skills are still developing and results will catch up. An unlucky veteran (35+) may be showing real physical decline, disguised as bad luck.

Conversely, a lucky young player might be developing genuine bat-to-ball skills. A lucky veteran is almost certainly riding a BABIP wave that will not last. Use the age filters below to explore these dynamics.

2025 Predictions vs. 2026 Reality

The real test of any predictive model: did the players flagged as unlucky in 2025 actually rebound in 2026? And did the lucky players regress? Here's what happened. 184 players appear in both datasets.

✓ Model Accuracy: 65%

Of the 46 players flagged as significantly unlucky or lucky in 2025, 30 moved in the predicted direction in 2026. That's meaningful signal. Statcast xwOBA is a genuine predictor of future performance, not just noise.

Biggest Rebound: Otto Lopez +80 pts

Otto Lopez (26) was 35 points below his xwOBA in 2025 (.295 actual vs .330 expected). In 2026 his wOBA jumped to .375, a gain of 80 points, exactly the correction the model predicted. Michael Harris II (+79 pts) and Adley Rutschman (+67 pts) followed the same pattern.

Biggest Regression: Cal Raleigh −140 pts

Cal Raleigh (28) was 21 points above his xwOBA in 2025 (.392 actual vs .371 expected). In 2026 his wOBA collapsed to .252, a drop of 140 points. TJ Friedl (−93 pts) and Zach McKinstry (−89 pts) also fell sharply after overperforming in 2025.

So What?
Luck Gaps Are Real, and They Predict Direction More Often Than Not

Among the 46 players flagged in 2025 with a luck gap beyond the ±0.015 threshold, 30 moved in the predicted direction in 2026: a 65% hit rate against a 50% random baseline. That 15-point improvement is meaningful for in-season roster decisions, particularly for waiver wire targets and trade candidates in the first half of a season.

The strongest individual calls: Otto Lopez (+0.080 wOBA), Michael Harris II (+0.079), and Adley Rutschman (+0.067) all rebounded sharply after being flagged as unlucky in 2025. On the regression side, Cal Raleigh (-0.140), TJ Friedl (-0.093), and Trea Turner (-0.081) each pulled back significantly after posting luck gaps above +0.020. The Raleigh case is particularly instructive: even elite contact quality does not sustain when BABIP runs significantly above expectation.

The 35% failure rate is worth contextualizing: this model predicts direction only, not magnitude. The practical implication is to treat Statcast luck gaps as a probabilistic filter, not a guarantee, and to weight the signal more heavily for prime-age players (26 to 30) where the noise-to-signal ratio is lowest.

Glossary

Statcast Impact Analysis
Statcast
MLB's tracking system in all 30 ballparks since 2015. Uses radar and cameras to measure every batted ball, pitch, and player movement with high precision.
xwOBA
Expected Weighted On-Base Average. Like wOBA but based on quality of contact (exit velocity + launch angle) rather than actual outcomes. Removes luck from results.
wOBA
Weighted On-Base Average. Weights each outcome (walk, single, double, HR) by its actual run value. More accurate than OPS. League average ~.320.
xBA
Expected Batting Average. Predicted batting average based on exit velocity and launch angle, ignoring defensive positioning and luck.
xSLG
Expected Slugging Percentage. Predicted slugging based on quality of contact metrics, independent of where the ball actually landed.
Exit Velocity
Speed of the ball off the bat in mph. Higher exit velocity correlates strongly with offensive production. Elite hitters average 95+ mph.
Launch Angle
Vertical angle at which the ball leaves the bat. Optimal for extra-base hits: 10-30 degrees. Negative = ground ball, 45+ degrees = pop-up.
Hard Hit%
Percentage of batted balls with exit velocity of 95+ mph. A direct proxy for consistent quality of contact.
BABIP
Batting Average on Balls In Play. Excludes home runs and strikeouts. League average ~.300. Extreme values often indicate luck rather than true skill.
Luck Gap
Actual wOBA minus expected xwOBA. A large positive gap suggests luck-driven performance; a large negative gap suggests bad luck with regression likely ahead.
Regression to the Mean
The statistical tendency for extreme performances to move back toward average over time. Players with large luck gaps tend to regress the following season.
